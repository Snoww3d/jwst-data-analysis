# May 3: Songs You Heard Once and the AI That Remembers Them

A Sunday conversation about using ChatGPT's music integrations to surface songs from 1999–2010 that were briefly on the radio and then forgotten — one of those use cases that highlights what AI recall does that search can't.

<!-- more -->
<!-- enriched -->

## Developer Journal

### The use case search couldn't solve

A friend was trying to solve a specific problem: reconstructing a playlist of hip-hop songs that were on the radio in the early 2000s — heard once or twice, never Shazam'd (Shazam didn't exist yet), and now inaccessible because the memory is too vague to search for.

Traditional music discovery doesn't help with this. Spotify and Apple Music recommend based on what you've played. Search requires at least a fragment of a title or artist. YouTube's algorithm optimizes for content you've engaged with, not content you've almost-but-not-quite forgotten.

ChatGPT's integrations with Apple Music and Spotify turn out to be surprisingly good at this: describe the era, the radio station type, the vibe — and it produces a playlist that includes songs you've heard recently on TikTok alongside ones you forgot had ever been in your memory. The friend found several that matched exactly.

### Why this matters for thinking about AI use cases

The JWST project is almost entirely in the "complex task execution" bucket for AI use — composite generation logic, security analysis, multi-file refactors. This playlist case is a good reminder that some of the highest-value AI use cases are in recall and curation rather than generation.

The session limits and token costs that came up earlier this week are shaped entirely by compute-intensive coding work. The memory/recall side of AI is cheap and nearly instant. The tool and use case need to match the task.

Note to self: might be worth an AI-assisted playlist exercise before the next long coding session. There is, as observed, going to be a need for work playlists eventually.
